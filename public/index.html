Chapter I
The Sinking Flagon
◆ ◇ ◆

Rain hammered the warped shingles of The Sinking Flagon, a roadside tavern half-swallowed by the moors outside the mining town of Halverton. Three strangers had ended up there by separate roads — a dwarf seeking work in the mines, a gnome chasing rumours of pre-Sundering ruins, and a drifter who didn't talk about where he came from.

The tavern door banged open. A mud-caked miner staggered in, blood running from a gash above his eye. He collapsed against the bar.

"They're gone," he gasped. "The night shift — all twelve of them. Something opened up in Tunnel Seven. We heard screaming, then... nothing."

The barkeep set down a bottle with a hard clack. Fifty gold to anyone who'd go down there and bring her people back. The Company wouldn't send help until morning. By then it would be too late.

Grimbold slammed his tankard down and stood, chainmail clanking. "Fifty gold be damned — those are miners down there, lass." He fixed the injured man with a hard stare. "I've worked deeper holes than you've seen nightmares. If something's opened up below, we go now."

In the far corner, Fūsetsu sat still as held breath. He had said nothing since arriving. His eyes missed nothing. Perception — Natural 20 + 3 = 23

The miner's hands were shaking, but not just from fear. His pupils were dilated wrong, one wider than the other. He kept glancing at the back door. He was not telling everything. And there was dark mud on his boots — almost black, with a faint iridescent sheen. Whatever was down in Tunnel Seven, he'd tracked some of it back.

One more thing. A fourth person sat in the far corner booth, hooded, nursing a drink, who had gone very still when the miner mentioned the ground opening up.

Grimbold challenged the stranger. A long silence. Then a woman's voice, low and measured.

"You don't want to go down there."

She pulled back her hood. Human, mid-forties, weathered face, a jagged scar running from temple to jaw. "Name's Voss." A surveyor for the Ironcradle Mining Consortium — the competition. She'd found the same necromantic mud seeping out of a collapsed shaft two valleys over. Three miners had gone missing there too. They were found eventually. Walking around in the dark. Not alive.

"I'm going down into Tunnel Seven tonight. With or without you."

◆ ◇ ◆

The rain hit like cold needles the moment the tavern door opened. The mud path up the ridge was slick and miserable, but short. Within ten minutes the mine entrance loomed ahead — sagging timbers framing a throat of darkness. A rusted cart track disappeared inside. The air coming out smelled of wet stone, iron, and something faintly sweet that had no business being underground.

A weathered sign read: SILVERBREAK MINE — TUNNEL 7: CLOSED BY ORDER OF FOREMAN HARSK.

Hierophantis placed his hand on Fūsetsu's shoulder and spoke the words of the Blessing of the Trickster. The rogue's edges blurred, shadow clinging to him like a second skin. Then the party formed up — Fūsetsu on point, Grimbold second with Light blazing on his shield, Voss third, Hierophantis fourth, and Nyx with the trembling miner bringing up the rear.

At the junction, the miner froze. His lantern trembled.

"That's... that's where we heard it. The singing."

Right now, it was silent.

Left fork: clicking sounds. Right fork: rhythmic breathing.

They went right.

The Nest

Grimbold's jaw tightened. The dwarf had no answer — because there wasn't one. He did kill their mother. They all did. And now he stood guard over the orphans he'd helped make.

"Take them. Do what you will. Give them names. Don't call me when you have more blood on your hands."

Chapter III
The Dead Who Wrote
◆ ◇ ◆

Sprocket's clockwork legs ticked against stone as the mechanical mouse scurried ahead into the bone chamber. Through the familiar bond, Nyx saw the glint resolve into detail: a silver locket on a tarnished chain, half-buried among scattered coins. Eight silver pieces lay among the bones, and tucked against the ribcage of one of the dead miners sat a sealed leather scroll case, still intact.

Two bodies. Mining clothes rotted to scraps. The collapse behind them blocked two-thirds of the passage. A gap remained at the top, maybe two feet wide. Cold air seeped through it.

Fūsetsu knelt beside the bones, methodical and unhurried. Investigation 22 Two miners — one larger, one smaller. The smaller one's hands were wrapped in torn cloth, fingers worn to bone — he had been digging at the rubble. Desperately trying to get through. A leather belt on the larger skeleton held an empty waterskin and a stub of charcoal pencil.

No wounds. No claw marks. They starved or suffocated after the blast sealed them in.

The locket clicked open easily. Inside: a tiny painted portrait of a young woman holding an infant, and a pressed lock of auburn hair. The back was engraved:

"For Corwin — come home safe. — Maren."

He didn't.

One detail more. The charcoal pencil matched faint markings scratched into the cave floor. Letters, barely visible under dust. Fūsetsu brushed them clear:

DREST KNOWS

A dying man's shaking hand, trying to write Drekk. He ran out of strength before he could finish.

Fūsetsu swept up the silver, the scroll case, and the locket in one fluid motion. He pocketed them without ceremony. The locket would wait. Everything waited, down here in the dark.

Chapter IV
Violet Veins
◆ ◇ ◆

The gap was tight — rough stone edges scraping against armour and packs — but passable. One by one, the party squeezed through. What opened beyond stole the breath.

A vast natural cavern stretched out, its ceiling lost in shadow high above. Water dripped in a slow rhythm, pooling in shallow standing water across the uneven floor. Rusted rail tracks emerged from the rubble and ran deeper into the darkness, flanked by abandoned mining equipment: overturned carts, scattered picks, a wheelbarrow on its side. This wasn't exploration — this was operation. Months of organised work.

But the light caught something wrong. Veins of violet-black mineral threaded through the cavern walls, pulsing with a faint, sickly luminescence. Not silver. Not anything they'd seen before. The glow cast strange shadows that seemed to shift when you weren't looking directly at them.

Nyx's eyes went wide. She pressed her fingers to the vein and jerked them back — ice-cold, tingling, radiating energy. She scraped flakes into her component pouch with trembling, excited hands.

Hierophantis knew what it was. The seminary texts had called it crystallised necrosis — ambient death energy compressed over centuries in deep earth. Shadowstone. Rare. Dangerous. And valued by certain cults for its resonance with necrotic energy.

If that was what these miners had stumbled onto, they'd found something far more dangerous than silver — and far more valuable to the wrong people.

The veins pulsed. The party pressed deeper.

Chapter VI
The Seal and the Source
◆ ◇ ◆

They found the reliquaries deep beneath the world, in chambers that predated the Consortium by centuries. Ancient dwarven work — not the rough-hewn practicality of mine shafts, but something deliberate and sacred.

Reliquary Three was empty. A stone plinth carved with angular geometric patterns, a circular depression worn smooth on top. Whatever it had held was gone. But someone had been here recently — a kicked-over lantern, a bedroll, a leather satchel half-submerged in a shallow pool. A sketch map marked this chamber and noted the plinth with a single annotation: "Removed to Director — see H."

Reliquary Two held more answers and darker ones. Landers' journal — the handwriting starting precise and deteriorating across the entries:

"Day 5. Torvin's wound isn't healing. Crystal forming IN the cut. Not infection — replacement."

"Day 8. Harlan won't stop humming. Says he can hear music from Rel. 1. Nobody else hears it."

The last entry was barely legible, scratched in shaking letters:

"Drest sealed the shaft. Harlan walked into Rel. 1 singing. Came back but it isn't him. The crystal GROWS FASTER near the plinth where the artifact was removed. The seal is FEEDING — we took its food so it's eating US instead."

The party seated the artifact back onto its plinth. Quarter-rotation. Click. The crystal veins dimmed, the pressure eased, and for the first time in weeks the resonance slowed.

R2 — restored.

◆ ◇ ◆

Reliquary One was worse.

Harlan stood in the centre of the chamber — if what remained could still be called standing. His body had crystallised from the feet up, violet-black stone replacing flesh in a slow wave that had reached his chest. His arms were locked at his sides. His eyes were open and had stopped working weeks ago. His mouth moved constantly, shaping a song no human throat should sustain.

His crystallised head turned toward Hierophantis. Only Hierophantis.

The brand on the cleric's wrist blazed with recognition.

◇

Fūsetsu acted. He took his oil flask and hurled it at Harlan's crystallised torso. Glass shattered against stone-flesh, oil splashing across faceted crystal. He struck flint and steel, and without looking back, flicked the lit tinder toward the oil-soaked figure.

"Time's up."

Grimbold lunged — Athletics 17 — but Fūsetsu's reflexes were faster. Acrobatics 18 The lit tinder left his fingers, spinning through the violet dark.

Then Hierophantis spoke.

His voice cracked through the chamber like a thunderclap — not words, not prayer, something older. Thaumaturgy ripped from his throat and the tiny flame on the spinning tinder guttered, dimmed, and died — snuffed to a cold ember three feet from Harlan's oil-slicked chest.

The tinder bounced off crystal. Cold. Dead. Harmless.

But the Source heard him.

The violet pulse lurched. Harlan's jaw stretched impossibly wide. The singing spiked a full octave — the entire chamber vibrating in response. Every crystal-threaded form along the walls went rigid, pointing toward the cleric like compass needles finding north.

"HARLAAAAAAN!!!" Fūsetsu yelled as he watched the flame spin in slow motion, falling from Grimbold's tackle. He looked up. The flint was only silver smoke. "The priest... what the—"

"Bloody hell, priest — whatever you just did, they all felt it!" Grimbold positioned himself between the crystals and his companions. "Nobody else move a muscle 'til we figure out if we're about to become decoration on these walls!"

Fūsetsu placed Grimbold's shield on the stone floor and angled it — concave face aimed at the Source, positioned to catch and focus whatever came back. He looked at the priest and gave him the same nod the priest had once given him.

Hierophantis closed his eyes. Placed his palms together, elbows out, stretched — the exact stance of Sunday morning mass, second nature since childhood. He breathed in and chanted from the soul. Not in Common this time. In Celestial. Beautiful, calming, harmonising. The way it was meant to be spoken.

Religion — d20 + 5 + Guidance d4 = 25

Celestial flooded the chamber. The ancient language poured through the parabolic curve of the shield and the cavern answered. Every crystal needle along the walls chimed — not the Source's hungry pulse, but something older. Something that remembered what it was. Harlan's singing shifted key, harmonising with the Celestial syllables as if the words were always meant to be part of the song.

The violet light on the plinth dimmed to a soft, steady glow. And in that steadiness, the harmonic channel opened wide — not sound anymore, but meaning. Images rippled across the crystal surfaces like memories trapped in glass: a robed figure kneeling at the plinth, willingly binding itself as conduit. Three reliquaries pulsing in triangulated rhythm. Centuries of patient channelling. Then — pickaxes. Artifacts pried free. The conduit starving, reaching upward through stone, feeding on anything with a spark of life.

The Source was not a prisoner. It was a guardian — and the Consortium had starved it.

The brand on Hierophantis's wrist blazed white, visible to everyone. The crystals nearest the shield leaned toward him like iron filings drawn to a magnet — growing, stretching, inching closer along the stone floor.

Harlan's crystallised head turned fully toward the cleric. What remained of his mouth shaped a single word in Celestial.

"Return."

Modern boot prints with violet residue showed someone else had been through recently. The passages led upward, eventually, to daylight and fresh air and a town called Halverton.

They emerged blinking into the light, carrying their dwarf between them.

Chapter VIII
The Bell Rings
◆ ◇ ◆

Morning came to Halverton like a reluctant promise. The party had rested at The Sheaf & Stone — the tavern they'd emerged near, run by a heavyset woman named Marta who asked hard questions and offered twenty gold per head. The night before, while the others slept, Fūsetsu had slipped through the common room in silence and found a lockbox behind the bar. Inside: a Consortium ledger marked "Halverton — Final Accounting," sealed parchments bearing Harken's orders, a coin pouch of twenty-five gold intended as Pastor Ollen's silence money, an iron key stamped with the Consortium's pickaxe-and-coin, and a charcoal map of the mine tunnels beneath their feet.

He also found the trapdoor. Under a rug, near the bar. Heavy iron, padlocked with the Consortium seal. The key fit. A second entrance to the mines — separate from Tunnel Seven.

But that was last night. This morning, Hierophantis had other plans.

◇

The chapel had been abandoned since the Consortium arrived. Dust on the pews. Cobwebs in the bell tower. Aldric, the old miner who served as warden, stood in the doorway with tears already forming before a word was spoken.

Hierophantis cast Thaumaturgy and his voice swelled threefold — the words booming off cracked stone, resonant and impossibly full, as if the building itself spoke. Candle flames flared bright and danced in unison. A dozen townsfolk stood in the doorway and between the pews, wide-eyed. Some knelt.

"Dear friends, come. There is only one path that leads us to the heavens. Light is the only guide, and faith your indisputable vessel. Come, let us spread the love, the empathy, the penance. Let us regain common cause, rebuild, restructure, recall what makes us alive."

The Celestial phrases hung in the air like music none of them understood but all of them felt.

"He actually sounds like he means it," a young man whispered.

Then Hierophantis reached for the bell rope.

BONG.

The sound hit like a fist — a deep iron voice that hadn't spoken in months. Dust shook loose from the rafters. The candle flames guttered and recovered. The note rolled out through the open door and across Halverton's empty lanes, echoing off stone and timber.

BONG. BONG.

Somewhere outside, a dog barked. A shutter creaked open.

Aldric stood frozen, tears cutting tracks through the grime on his cheeks. He said nothing. He didn't need to.

The chapel bell rang over Halverton for the first time since the Consortium arrived.

Chapter IX
The Cutting Room
◆ ◇ ◆

The smithy's rear workshop was dark. No windows, forge cold, the bellows and anvil shoved aside. Scattered around it: specialised chisels with darkened edges, a modified saw with violet-stained teeth, heavy leather gloves blackened at the fingertips. Against the back wall, a shelf held a leather-bound logbook and stacked work orders.

This wasn't storage. This was a cutting room. The Consortium had been processing shadowstone right here in Halverton.

The logbook held shipping manifests — refined material sent to "V.H., Thornwall" at accelerating intervals. References to "E.K." and a "Thornwall lab." A parchment sketch labelled "resonance bridge." Drag marks at the rear door — a second boulder had been removed recently.

The brand on his wrist flared. Warm. The same heat from the fissure, from the Source. The scar tissue glowed faintly beneath his skin, matching the boulder's rhythm exactly.

His eyes kept darting to the smithy workshop door. He knew what was inside. He was terrified someone else had been in there.

Fūsetsu confronted him with the evidence. The initials. The logbook. The resonance bridge. Edric broke.

"My men are still down there. Three of them. Drekk sealed the shaft and left them to rot. That was three weeks ago."

His hand went to the chisel at his belt — not threatening, just gripping it like a lifeline.

"If you know about the workshop, you saw the bridge notes. I was trying to reach them. The stone — it resonates through the rock. I thought if I could tune it right, I could break through the sealed gate without collapsing the shaft."

He swallowed hard.

"It's not working. And the stone... it's getting worse. Louder."

Then Fūsetsu told him what they'd seen below. The crystallisation. Harlan's body, standing rigid, singing a song that never stopped. Stone replacing flesh from the feet up.

"Crystallised." The word came out flat, dead. "Three weeks I've been trying to reach them, and they're... they're turning into that stuff?"

He stared at the pulsing boulder visible through the workshop door. Something shifted behind his eyes.

"The resonance experiments. I was making it worse, wasn't I."

It wasn't really a question.

Chapter XI
The Man in the Cellar
◆ ◇ ◆

Edric spotted the watcher first — a curtain dropping back into place in an upper window, two buildings north. Then boot prints from the well, through the alley, to a root cellar on Halverton's eastern edge. Marta's old cold storage.

Edric stayed topside with his hammer. The party descended.

They found him pressed against the far wall. Male, average height, leather jerkin, hooded. His name was Marsh. A cutting floor worker — processing, not mining. Left behind when Drekk pulled everyone out. No cart, no pay, no explanation. He'd been moving refined shadowstone from hidden caches to a dead drop outside town for a Thornwall pickup, desperately hoping someone would eventually pay him.

With Grimbold's axe and Hierophantis's Light-blazing ceremonial dagger filling the cellar with white fire and steel, Marsh talked. He talked about everything.

The dead drop — a hollow oak stump, half a mile north on the Thornwall road. The two remaining caches — one under the smithy floorboards, one in the granary cellar. The specialist from Thornwall — a woman with clean hands and fancy tools who took twelve refined crystal samples in a lead-lined case and showed no interest in what was happening to the miners. Only the product.

And Harlan. Marsh had watched Harlan change.

"He stopped wearing boots. Walked barefoot on cold stone and didn't shiver. Stopped talking. Drekk said he was 'acclimating.' Edric tried to pull him off rotation — Drekk overruled."

"Torvin stopped eating. But he didn't lose weight."

The stone was feeding him.

Under pressure, Marsh whispered a name he clearly wished he hadn't.

"Director Halmund."

Not Harken. Someone above Harken. Someone Marsh was more afraid of than anything in this cellar.

Fūsetsu studied the man the entire time. Insight — Natural 20 + 5 = 25 Genuinely scared. Genuinely truthful. A small man caught in a machine far larger than himself.

The rogue sheathed his swords. Then, just for fun, he slapped his right hand on Marsh's shoulder and leaned in close.

"Boo."

Marsh flinched so hard he nearly cracked his skull on the wall. A strangled yelp escaped before he caught himself, breathing ragged. The swords were sheathed. The threat had shifted to something worse — casual amusement.

"Marsh, you just landed yourself an accompanied ride to Thornwall."

◆ ◇ ◆

Grimbold hauled Marsh up the cellar stairs by the arm. Hierophantis followed, Cyric's dagger still blazing. Nyx trailed behind, embarrassed — she'd tried to cast Light on a copper piece and the copper had stayed stubbornly dim. She didn't know that cantrip.

"Lass, put that copper away — you're embarrassing yourself. And you, priest — keep that blade handy."

Nyx stuffed the copper back into her component pouch.

"Right, yes, well — Light is a simple transmutation, basic principle of luminous energy manipulation, but I suppose my focus was elsewhere with all the interrogation and— never mind!"

Fūsetsu emerged into daylight first. Noon sun overhead. The broken cart thirty paces away.

And Edric Kessler standing at the cellar entrance with his hammer, staring at the man being dragged up the stairs. A man he recognised from the Consortium operation. Same shift as Harlan.

He didn't know yet what Marsh had said about his men. He was about to find out.

Chapter XII
The Trail to Thornwall
◆ ◇ ◆

At The Sheaf & Stone, Fūsetsu laid it all out for Marta. The broodmother in the deep. The miners turning to stone. Pastor Ollen's betrayal — five gold a week for silence while his flock suffered. Marsh as the dead drop runner. Three keystones that held the seal beneath the convergence vein. Two restored. One taken.

"The trail leads to Thornwall. Harken, the missing keystone, Ollen — all of it."

Nyx leaned forward, eyes bright behind smudged spectacles.

"We have to get to Thornwall and retrieve that keystone before whatever's sealed below breaks free completely!"
